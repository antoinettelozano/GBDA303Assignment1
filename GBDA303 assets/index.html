Homicides in Toronto Neighbourhoods: Reflection

This study focuses on the homicide rates across different neighbourhood classifications in Toronto from 2020 to 2023. By categorizing neighbourhoods into Neighborhood Improvement Areas (NIA), Emerging Neighbourhoods (EN), and Not an Emerging or Improvement Area (NA), we can get a better understanding of the distribution and underlying factors contributing to homicides in Toronto.

 

Data Analysis

 

In this study, I designed an infographic using Adobe Illustrator and a detailed map using Mapbox to show patterns of homicides in the City of Toronto. Using datasets from the “Police Annual Statistical Report - Homicides” and “Neighbourhoods” available on Toronto’s open data portal, I determined the number of communities in each neighbourhood classification and matched this with the locations of the homicides to see how many homicides occurred in each neighbourhood classification.

 

When initially analyzing the data, I noticed significantly more NA neighbourhoods compared to EN and NIA, resulting in a higher total number of homicides in the NA category. This could paint a misleading image that these were the most dangerous areas based on raw numbers alone. To provide a more accurate understanding, I calculated the homicide rates for each neighbourhood classification, focusing on the rates instead of solely total homicides.

 

The data shows that NIAs have the highest homicide rate at 2.91 per neighbourhood (96 homicides in 33 neighbourhoods), ENs have a rate of 2.5 (25 homicides in 10 neighbourhoods), and NAs have the lowest rate at 1.55 (178 homicides in 115 neighbourhoods). NIAs face higher crime, lower income, and fewer resources, contributing to higher homicide rates. ENs, in transition with ongoing development, have moderate rates. NAs, with better infrastructure and resources, have the lowest rates. These socio-economic factors drive the varying homicide rates across classifications.

 

Design Rationale

 

The poster design aims to communicate complex data in a clear, visually appealing format. Each neighbourhood classification is reflected by a distinct house graphic to differentiate the data visually. This helps viewers quickly understand the distribution and comparison across categories. Additionally, my use of vertical bars with labels and a legend allows easy visual understanding. 

 

Pie graphs with icons representing shootings, stabbings, and other causes of homicides provide a breakdown of homicide types, helping the reader understand the nature of homicides in Toronto. Finally, the map displays a visual, geographic distribution of homicides, enhancing understanding of crime distribution and identifying hotspots for targeted interventions.

 

Real-World Applications

 

The insights from this data can inform policymakers and city planners in Toronto about the areas most affected by homicides. This information is critical for allocating resources effectively, such as increasing police presence and investing in community programs in high-risk areas like NIAs and ENs. Non-profit organizations and community groups can use this data to target interventions in the neighbourhoods most in need. 

 

Urban planners can leverage this data to understand the socio-economic factors contributing to high homicide rates in specific areas. Development projects can then incorporate safety features, better infrastructure, and community spaces designed to reduce crime. This data can also be used in public awareness campaigns to educate residents about safety issues in their neighbourhoods.

 

The poster and map effectively visualizes homicide data in Toronto, revealing homicide rates across different neighbourhood classifications. My design choices ensure clarity and impact, making the information accessible for government officials, urban planners, and community residents, supporting vulnerable communities and a safer community.
